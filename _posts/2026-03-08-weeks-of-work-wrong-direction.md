The first thing I did was start designing screens.

I had a clear picture in my head of what the app should look like — a weekly calendar, assistant cards, a clean dashboard with summary numbers — and I started describing it to Claude and ChatGPT. Both were helpful. Maybe too helpful. They generated layouts, suggested colour palettes, proposed interaction patterns. I refined things. I iterated. I went deep.

I built out the calendar view in detail — not code, but a clickable prototype. I worked on how an assistant's profile card should look. I mapped out a flow for how an assistant would log their hours at the end of a shift, and what would happen if their entry got rejected, and how they'd resubmit it. There were several screens for that flow alone.

I spent probably two or three weeks in this space. The thing I was building kept getting more detailed, so I kept feeling like I was making progress.

A few weeks in, I sat down with my husband — who fills in the FK forms with me every month — and walked him through what I'd made. He looked at it carefully. He asked a few questions. Then he said, more or less: the scheduling part looks very thought-through, but I can't see where the forms actually get filled in.

He was right. I had spent weeks designing the parts that felt interesting to design and had left almost untouched the part of the app that would solve the actual problem. The forms. The hour reporting. The monthly submission. Everything I was building around was there in detail, but the centre was missing.

The wireframes weren't worthless — some of the thinking survived into the real build. But I'd gone a long way down a road without checking where it led.

What I should have done first: had Claude read Försäkringskassan's actual documentation. Understood the forms before designing anything. What fields exist, what counts as reportable time, how the two forms relate to each other. None of that was in my prototypes because I hadn't looked at it yet. I was designing a solution to a problem I hadn't read.

Going back to the beginning was uncomfortable in the specific way that backtracking always is. But the second attempt was more grounded, because it started from the right place.
